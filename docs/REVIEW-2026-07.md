## 2. Corrections required

### 2.1 The 20% LTCG breakpoint is stale
`LTCG_HIGH_THRESHOLD: 550000` — the 2026 breakpoint is **$613,700 MFJ / $533,400 single**. At defaults the balloon-year taxable proxy is ~$883k; the higher MFJ threshold moves ~$64k of gain from 20% back to 15% (~$3.2k to the seller). Make it a filing-status-driven constant.

### 2.2 OBBBA charitable floor (effective 2026) is missing
Itemizers now deduct charitable contributions only above **0.5% of AGI, per year claimed**. Across the six shield years (aggregate AGI ≈ $1.7M at defaults) roughly $8–9k of the $440k deduction is disallowed — ~$2–3k federal cost. Small but the model currently claims a deduction the Code no longer allows in full. (The 35% top-bracket cap does not bind a 24%-bracket seller.)

### 2.3 Investor exit tax is understated — the single largest error found
The engine taxes all exit recapture at 25%. The $408k of Year-1 bonus sits on **§ 1245 property** (cost-seg carve-out + 15/5-yr CapEx); its recapture is **ordinary income at the investor's 35% rate**, not 25%. Bounds, re-run through the engine:

| Exit-recapture treatment | Exit tax | REPS IRR |
|---|---|---|
| All at 25% (current model) | $117k | 9.49% |
| Full § 1245 ordinary on $408k short-life | $158k | 6.53% |

Reality lands between: § 1245 recapture is capped at the gain *allocated to those assets*, and five-year-old appliances/site improvements support a low exit allocation. A defensible purchase-price allocation at takeout is worth roughly 1.5–2.5 IRR points — it is a negotiated schedule in the co-op buyout agreement, not an afterthought. Model it as an input (`exitShortLifeAllocationPct`); default the blended exit tax to ~$135k, REPS IRR ~8%.

### 2.4 Ohio Business Income Deduction — overlooked, and it pays the seller ~$18k
ORC 5747.01(B) defines business income to include gain from the complete or partial liquidation of a business. Rental-activity income and the gain on disposition of the rental property should qualify for the **BID: first $250k/yr deducted, 3% flat above**. Re-run: seller's Ohio tax drops from **$34,796 → $16,738** (only the balloon year exceeds $250k). Investors' small positive years ($19–24k) fall entirely inside the BID — Ohio tax ≈ $0 in profit years. This needs the CPA to confirm business-income characterization (it is the Department's published position for Schedule E rentals operated as a trade or business), but it is the cheapest $18k in the deal. It also stacks with the seller-priority ordering: the installment structure is what keeps four of five years under the $250k BID ceiling.

### 2.5 Basis allocation: this is not a § 1011(b) bargain sale
The engine apportions *total* adjusted basis pro-rata by FMV share. But the land is donated to the **CLT** and the improvements are sold to the **co-op** — two assets, two counterparties, no bargain element in either leg. Asset-level basis controls: land basis = $62.5k (25% of cost, never depreciated); building adjusted basis = $187.5k − $185k = **$2.5k**. Consequences: GPR rises from 95.7% to ~99.8% (slightly more gain per dollar collected — modestly worse for the seller), and the charitable deduction leg is unaffected (FMV deduction regardless of basis, since long-term § 1231/capital property). The pro-rata method would only be right if the seller bargain-sold a single property to one charity. Fix the allocation; the current version overstates seller after-tax by ~$5–8k NPV.

### 2.6 Housekeeping
NIIT/filing status hard-codes MFJ ($250k); expose filing status. Seller-side straight-line-only depreciation history must be confirmed (already flagged — keep the flag; § 453(i) would bite if any § 1245 property exists on the seller's schedule). The land-donation § 170 opinion, qualified appraisal, and Form 8283 remain mandatory. Confirm no step-transaction exposure: the donation should not be contractually contingent on the co-op sale closing.

## 3. Yellow Springs cost realism — the defaults are too round and too low

### 3.1 Water/sewer: the highest rates in the Miami Valley, verified
Village of Yellow Springs water is **$18.04 per 1,000 gallons in 2026, rising to $19.48 in 2027** (Council-authorized 8%/yr increases through 2027), against a 1997 rate of $2.15. Combined water + sewer runs ≈ **$31.6 per 1,000 gallons** (the Piqua 70-utility survey puts YS at $710.19/quarter on 22,500 gal — the highest of every Miami Valley community; Fairborn averages $66/month, Xenia $38). The drivers are structural and not going away: a $6.7M treatment-plant loan amortized across only 1,927 accounts ($440k/yr, 20 years left), $2.5M of EPA-mandated galvanized line replacement, PFAS compliance due by 2032, and 28% system water loss.

For a master-metered 1968 complex at ~2,300 gal/unit/month: **~$870/unit/yr for water/sewer alone**, plus trash and common-area electric ≈ **$1,150/unit/yr**. The current default of $600/unit is roughly half of reality. This line also carries the steepest known escalator in the deal: 8%/yr locked through 2027, with PFAS capital almost certainly extending rate pressure beyond.

### 3.2 Insurance: $15,000 is a 2019 number
National multifamily insurance averaged **$777/unit in 2024** (up 55% from $502 in 2021), and habitational is now one of the hardest property lines — 30–40% of new placements are going to E&S paper. A 1968 garden-style asset draws underwriting scrutiny on wiring era (aluminum branch wiring was standard 1965–73 — verify and remediate; COPALUM/AlumiConn retrofits materially improve quotes), panels, and roof age. Realistic 2026 default: **$900/unit → $22,500/yr**, escalating ~8%/yr. Budgeting $15k invites a $10k+ hole in year one.

### 3.3 Property taxes: right level today, wrong assumption tomorrow
$28,472 ≈ 1.9% effective on $1.5M — consistent with Yellow Springs' 1.95% median effective rate, the highest bracket in Greene County. Two unmodeled realities: the 2024 county reappraisal moved YS values ~24%, and the recorded conveyance will hand the Auditor an arm's-length price. Default to **$30,000** with a ~3%/yr escalator, and note two live diligence items: a Board of Revision challenge if the reassessment lands above the improvements-only economics, and the (contested, case-by-case) Ohio charitable exemption for the CLT-owned land parcel.

### 3.4 R&M and reserves on a 58-year-old building
$900/unit R&M is thin for a 1968 vintage; $1,200/unit is the credible floor. Reserves at $300/unit is the HUD new-construction convention; older stock warrants **$400/unit** minimum.

### 3.5 The inflation gap is structural, not cosmetic
`Property taxes/insurance held flat across phases` understates Phase 2 badly. With the known escalators (water 8%, insurance ~8%, taxes ~3%), year-6 opex is ≈ $30k above the flat assumption, putting the true Phase-2 rent near **$885–900**, not $787. The engine needs per-line escalator inputs; the "Phase 2 rent falls 5%" story does not survive them.

## 4. What realism does to the rents — and the honest new story

| Scenario | Phase-1 rent | vs. current $700 |
|---|---|---|
| Current defaults | $711 | +1.6% |
| Realistic YS opex | **$824** | **+17.7%** |
| Realistic opex + accrued (PIK) pref | $742 | +6.0% |

The "structure works without rent increases" narrative does not survive real Yellow Springs water and 2026 insurance. The defensible story: **cost-recovery rent of ~$824, still below the $900–1,100 market comps** — affordability margin of $75–275/month, delivered with honest operating assumptions.

Tenant-side levers, in ROI order: (1) **water conservation CapEx** — at $31.6/1,000 gal, a single running toilet costs ~$2,300/yr; $15–20k of low-flow fixtures and leak detection (folded into the 5-yr CapEx bucket, 100% bonus-eligible) plausibly cuts the utility line 15–20%; (2) **pref structure** — accruing rather than current-paying the 7% pref holds Phase-1 rent at $742 with the same investor economics shifted to exit; a 4% current / 3% accrued split lands ~$783; (3) **insurance engineering** — higher deductibles, wiring remediation, documented roof CapEx; (4) **Phase-2 self-management** saves $15k/yr (the engine currently inflates management 15% instead); (5) BOR tax challenge and CLT-land exemption pursuit. Note that Yellow Springs already has an operating CLT — **YS Home, Inc.** — the natural partner and precedent-holder.

Investor-side IRR levers: § 199A QBI (20% deduction on the $19–24k positive years — unmodeled), the Ohio BID (zeroes state tax in profit years), negotiated exit purchase-price allocation (§ 2.3 — worth 1.5–2.5 IRR points), REPS status also exempts the rental income from NIIT, and closing timing (bonus needs placed-in-service in year one).
